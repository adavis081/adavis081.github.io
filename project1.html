Wine Review Analysis

Project Overview

This project is an exploratory analysis of a dataset containing reviews of thousands of wines. Wines span across various countries, regions, vineyards, and varietals. Lastly, every wine is given a score from 0-100. This project uses statistical analysis, natural language processing, and data visualization to explore the driving factors of a wine's score.

Results, Insights, and Lessons Learned

  1. Data Preparation:

    Considering we want to investigate how certain factors impact rating, we want to ensure that possible explanatory variables are clean. Some features are only missing a few values. With around 130,000 total observations, these can simply be dropped.

    However, price is missing a significant amount of data points. The distribution of price is heavily skewed as some wines are very expensive. These can be considered outliers and dropped to give price a normal distribution. We can then fill the missing values with the average price.

  2. Rating vs. Price:

    We can use statistical analysis techniques to examine if the price of a wine has any impact on its rating. First, we check the correlation value between the two features. With a correlation of about 0.45, there are no immediate signs of a relationship.

    To examine further, we create groups of wines based on their price using quartiles. Our null hypothesis is that the average rating of these groups are the same, and our alternative is that they are different. After conducting an ANOVA test, our p-value tells us that we cannot reject our null hypothesis. It appears that price has no obvious impact on the ratings.

  3. Rating vs. Description:

    Next, we will try to use natural language processing (NLP) to see what we can learn from the reviewer's descriptions as they relate to their scores.

    First, we clean the descriptions. To prepare the text for analysis, we remove punctuation, special characters, and stop words that generate noise in the data.

    First, we conduct a sort of 'eye test' by generating wordclouds for different segments of ratings. Since ratings are normally distributed, we split them down the middle. We then generate a wordcloud for both of these groups. When examining these word clouds, there are no obvious results. We see a similar make-up words describing wine that were scored both poorly and well. They seem purely descriptive and do not contain sentiment.

    Lastly, we build an NLP classifier to see if we can bin wines based on their rating. We split wines by rating using the quartiles of the rating distribution. We label these wines 'fair', 'good', 'great', and 'excellent'. Our TF-IDF Random Forest classifier predicts with a accuracy of about 60%. This result probably has little meaning as this model does not predict any category very well.

  4. Thoughts and Lessons Learned:

    Considering the results of this analysis, it does not appear that ratings can be explained by features in this dataset. There may be driving factors of these ratings, such as the conditions each wine was made in, but that would require another dataset. It is important to note that these ratings are given by a large community of reviewers. All of these reviewers have their own thoughts and preferences, and therefore are biased. With wine reviews being a highly subjective process, it may be that these ratings cannot be explained or modeled through data.

  5. Next Steps:

    If I were to continue this analysis, I would investigate the relationship between ratings and country of origin. However, there is a vast amount of countries and regions in this dataset. The wines reviewed come from countries all over the world. However countries like Italy and France will have many more observations than countries like China. In the end, it may not yield significant results.

    I am also interested in investigating additional datasets to explore other possible explanations of ratings.
