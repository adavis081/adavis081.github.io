Wine Reviews

Project Overview

This project is an exploratory analysis of a dataset containing reviews of thousands of wine. Wines span across various countries, regions, vineyards, and varietals. Lastly, every wine is given a score from (0-100). This project uses statistical analysis, natural language processing, and data visualization to explore the driving facotrs of a wine's score.

Results, Insights, and Thoughts

  1. Data Cleaning: Considering that we want to investigate how certain factors impact rating, we want to make sure these variables are clean and filled. Some variables only were missing a few values. With around 130K observations, these could simply be dropped.

    However, price was missing a significant amount of values. When checking the distribution of price, it was heavily skewed as some wines are very expensive. These were considered to be outliers, and dropped to give price a normal distribution. We can then fill the missing values with the average price.

  2. Price vs. Rating: We use statistical analysis techniques to examine if the price of a wine has any impact on its rating. First, we check the correlation score between the two variables. With a correlation score of about 0.45, there are no immediate signs of a relationship.

    On last check to see if ratings are different between different prices, we split up wines based on their price. Our null hypothesis is that the averge rating of these groups are the same, and our alternative is that they are different. After conducting an ANOVA test, our p-value tells us that we cannot reject our null hypothesis. It appears that price has no impact on ratings.

  3. Description vs. Rating: Can we use NLP to somehow relate rating with the description it gets from the reviewer? First, we clean the text in the description column. We remove punctuation, special characters, and stop words that generate noise in the data.

    First, we conduct a sort of 'eye test', by generating wordclouds for different segments of ratings. Since ratings are normally distributed, we split them down the middle, and generate two word clouds. When examining these word clouds, there are not any obvious results. We see similar words describing wine that were scored poorly vs. well. They are purely descriptive and do not contain sentiment.

    Lastly, we build an NLP classifier to see if we can bin wines based on their rating. We split wines by rating using the quartiles of the rating distribution. We label these wines 'fair', 'good', 'great', and 'excellent'. Our TF-IDF Random Forest classifier predicts with a accuracy of about 60%.

  4. Thoughts and Conclusion: Considering the results of the statistical analysis, the natural language processing, and wordclouds, it does not appear that ratings can be explained by this dataset. There may be driving factors that appear in other datasets, such as the conditions each wine was made in, but that would require another dataset. Lastly, it is important to note that ratings are given by a large community of reviews. All of these reviewers have their own thoughts and preferences, and therefore are biased. I wonder if wine rating can even be explained by a dataset similar to this one.

  5. What I Would Do Next: If I were to continue this analysis, I would investigate the relationship between ratings and countries. However, there is a vast amount of countries and regions in this dataset. With that in mind, this analysis would need to point in certain and specific direction. It would need to ask questions such as, "Is there a difference between the ratings of the United States, France, and Germany?"

    I would be interested in investigating other datasets to find out what really drives the ratings of wines, and if it can even be represented in a dataset.
